The fire burned bright and warm that evening, higher than usual, as if it too understood that something had changed. Behind you, down by the edge of the beach, the raft was finished: large, sturdy, and ready. The logs were bound together, the sail was stretched tight, the ropes pulled firm. What had been only a plan for so long now stood there for real.

For the first time in a long while, no one in the camp was working. No one carried firewood, no one checked the traps, no one twisted rope. Instead, you sat gathered around the fire with food in your hands and tired smiles on your faces. Someone laughed quietly. Someone else simply stared into the flames, as if it was still hard to understand that you had actually succeeded.

At last, you broke the silence.

"The raft is finished," you said. "We did what we said we would do."

Several people nodded. Someone raised their clay bowl slightly into the air. A few quiet cheers were heard, but they quickly faded again. Everyone knew that the work might be done.

The decision was not.

"We can sail now," said [person_1], looking toward the darkness where the sea lay. "We know the raft holds. We have food, water, sails, a map, and a compass. We can leave."

"Yes," said [person_2], "but we also know there were more people on the plane. We have found some of them, but not all. There could still be people out there in the jungle."

"But how long are we supposed to wait?" [person_3] objected. "We have waited a long time already. We have worked ourselves to the bone for this. If we stay too long, we might lose our chance. The weather could turn. Something could happen to the raft. The supplies will not last forever."

"But if we sail now and leave someone behind..." said [person_4] quietly, without finishing the sentence.

The words hung there between you.

The waves could be heard faintly from the beach. The fire cracked. Someone pulled their blanket tighter around their shoulders.

"We do not know how many survived the crash," someone else said. "We do not know if there are still more people out there. And we do not know, if there are, whether they can wait any longer."

No one had an easy answer. Everyone around the fire looked worn down, hardened by the sun, hunger, work, and fear. But you were something else now, too. You were no longer just people trying to survive from one day to the next. You were a group that had built something together.

And that was why the decision carried so much weight.

At last, [person_1] turned toward you.

"We have followed you this far," said [person_4]. "We would not be sitting here now if you had not kept us going when everything looked hopeless."

One by one, the others nodded.

"Someone has to make this decision," said [person_2]. "And it should be you."

No one protested. No one looked away.

Only the glow of the fire, the darkness of the night, and every gaze fixed on you.

The raft was finished.

Now all that remained was to decide whether you would sail toward rescue — or wait a little longer for those who might still be out there.
